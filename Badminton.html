Badminton -Your life Game

Badminton is a racquet game in which players hit a shuttlecock through a net with racquets. The most prevalent varieties of the sport are "singles" (of one player per side) and "doubles" (with 2 players per side). Badminton is frequently played as a casual outdoor sport in a yard or on the beach, with serious games taking place on a rectangular indoor court. The shuttlecock is struck with the racquet and landed within the other team's half of the court to earn points.

Court

The court is rectangular, with a net dividing it into two halves. Although badminton regulations allow a court to be marked for singles alone, most courts are marked for both singles and doubles play. The singles court is longer than the doubles court, although both are the same width. The only difference is that the doubles courts has a short serve-length dimension, which might be confusing for younger players.

Serving

Whenever the player serves, the shuttlecock should cross over the opponents' court's short service line to avoid a fault. Until the server strikes the shuttlecock, the server and receiver must stay within respective service courts, avoiding touching the boundary lines. The other two players are free to stand anywhere they like as long as they don't hinder the server's or receiver's eyesight.

Singles

Because only one person can covering the full court, singles strategies rely on driving the opposition to move as much as possible; this explains that singles strokes are typically directed to the court's corners. Lifts and clears are combined with drop shots and net shots to take use of the length of the court. Because the smasher has no companion to back up their effort and thus is vulnerable to a well-placed return, smashing is less common in singles than in doubles. Furthermore, in singles, when energy saving is important, frequent smashing may be taxing.

Doubles

Both teams will attempt to achieve and maintain an offensive advantage, smashing downward when the chance comes. When at all feasible, a pair will attack in the ideal offensive configuration, with one player smashing down from the back court and their partner intercepting all smashes returns save the lift. If the attacker in the back court hits a drop shot, their partner will advance into to the forecourt to threaten the net. If a couple is unable to hit downward, they will attempt to obtain the attack by using flat strokes. If a couple is forced to lift or clear the shuttlecock, they must defend: they will take up a side-by-side posture on the back midcourt, allowing them to cover the whole width of their court against the opponents' smashes.In doubles, players typically smash to the center area between two players to capitalize on confusion and conflicts.

Mixed Doubles

Both couples in mixed doubles often strive to maintain an offensive formation with the lady in front and the male in rear. This is due to the fact that male players are often much stronger and can thus create more powerful smashes. Mixed doubles, as a result, need better tactical awareness and finer positional play. Competitors who are astute will attempt to invert the optimum position by driving the woman to the back and the male to the front. Mixed players must be cautious and meticulous in their shot selection in order to avoid this threat.
